01 — origin

In 2016 Ludovico Einaudi played a grand piano on a floating platform in the Arctic Ocean, a glacier calving behind him, the sea around him strewn with broken ice.

Cold Piano is a translation of that image into scent — not the music, but the cold around the instrument: the wooden body breathing into freezing air, the metal of the strings, the ozone of glacial wind, and far below, the black mineral depth of the water.

It is built from twelve raw materials, kept under sixty euros a kilo, and calculated to comply with IFRA Category 4 at ten percent in alcohol. Everything that follows is the working of it — shown in full.

↗ Ludovico Einaudi — “Elegy for the Arctic” (Greenpeace)

05 — the cost

cost of the concentrate

€/kg

budget 60 €/kg · under

Cost is driven almost entirely by two materials used at 10%: Cypriol (the dark, smoky root, at €400/kg) and Ambroxan (the grey-amber base, at €500/kg). Together they make up of the cost.

The trace metallics — Aldehyde C12 MNA and Rose Oxide — are diluted to 0.1%, a tenth of the sheet’s reference, so their cost is scaled to a tenth and they contribute almost nothing.

06 — compliance

IFRA Category 4

Calculated for a fine fragrance dosed at 10% in alcohol, against the IFRA 51st Amendment. Concentration in the finished product = (parts × dilution ÷ 1000) × 10%.

compliant

No material in this formula carries a binding IFRA Category 4 quantitative limit. The most concentrated material in the finished eau de toilette is at — comfortably unrestricted. The only two materials with any published usage ceiling, Eucalyptol (0.30% of concentrate vs. ≈20%) and Aldehyde C12 MNA (0.004% vs. ≈2%), sit far beneath theirs. Verify against the current IFRA Standards Library before any commercial launch.
